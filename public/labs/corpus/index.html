the work

(to)complete, 2026

networked text, visitor-triggered web page

(to)complete borrows the promise of autocomplete: that the next word will finish the thought. Here, each accepted visit becomes a prompt, and the page answers by adding one small unit of language to a shared sentence. The work moves toward completion by making itself less complete.

The piece sits between two old desires: to let language classify the world, and to let a machine predict what should come next. Its grammar is rule-bound but unstable, cumulative but unresolved. There is nothing to type or submit; attention is the input.

privacy

Each addition is recorded in an open event journal. To prevent accidental repeats, the system keeps only short hashed traces of the session and network address. It stores no raw addresses, full user agents, fingerprints, or analytics. The visit matters as an event, not as an identity.

archival

The live page is the work, but it is not a permanent object. It depends on a host, a worker, a domain, maintenance, and time. The journal records its additions, and the static snapshot freezes one state for citation. These layers preserve evidence of the work, not the work itself; to archive it completely would be to end the condition that keeps it unfinished.
